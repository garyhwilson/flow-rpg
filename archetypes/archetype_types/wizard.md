# Wizard
*Master of arcane knowledge and magical manipulation*

## Core Identity

The Wizard represents the mastery of arcane knowledge and magical manipulation through disciplined study, analytical understanding, and methodical experimentation. While many individuals might possess latent magical sensitivity or limited ritual knowledge, true Wizards distinguish themselves through systematic approach to arcane forces, treating magic as both an academic discipline and practical art worthy of lifelong devotion. They transform intuitive magical perception into refined arcane capability through rigorous study, documented experimentation, and theoretical framework development.

- **Magical Role:** Arcane specialist, knowledge seeker, and magical researcher
- **Key Attributes:** Mind, Awareness, Will
- **Magical Focus:** Analytical spellcasting, arcane theory, and systematic magical application

## Mechanical Foundations

### Key Attributes
Wizards typically develop attribute distributions emphasizing mental capability, perceptive awareness, and disciplined will:

**Mind:** Primary attribute supporting analytical understanding, theoretical development, and spell formulation. Contributes to magical complexity management, interdisciplinary integration, and arcane innovation.

**Awareness:** Essential for magical pattern recognition, energy manipulation, and effect perception. Contributes to subtle energy detection, spell component identification, and arcane interaction assessment.

**Will:** Critical for maintaining spell integrity, focusing magical intention, and resisting arcane backlash. Supports extended casting endurance, spell stability maintenance, and interference rejection.

**Presence:** Varies based on magical tradition, with some emphasizing projection capability for certain spell types while others focus more on internal discipline than external expression.

**Grace:** Useful for precise somatic components and delicate manipulation required by certain spell formulations. Supports specific casting techniques requiring physical precision rather than merely mental focus.

**Might:** Typically receives less emphasis than other attributes, though specific traditions incorporate physical resilience training to counteract the bodily strain of extended magical manipulation.

### Starting Skills

Wizards begin with the following skill proficiencies, reflecting their specialized training combining arcane practice with scholarly research:

**Professional (+2):**
- Sorcery (magical theory, energy manipulation, and spell formulation)
- Lore (academic knowledge, research methodology, and historical context)

**Novice (-1):**
- Investigate (detailed observation, pattern recognition, and experimental design)

**Special Ability:**
- **Arcane Spellcasting:** Can cast arcane spells regardless of attributes (archetype training overrides normal spellcasting requirements)

**Additional Skills:**
- Choose three additional skills at Basic/Untrained level reflecting background and cultural training

Wizards are typically Untrained in heavy physical activity, deceptive arts, or wilderness survival unless their background specifically includes exposure to these areas.

### Flow Mechanics

Wizards interact with the Flow resource through disciplined magical manipulation:

**Flow Generation:**
- Successfully casting complex spells with precise execution (+1 Flow)
- Identifying magical patterns or phenomena through observation (+1 Flow)
- Solving arcane puzzles or theoretical challenges (+1 Flow)
- Applying magical knowledge to practical problems (+1 Flow)
- Achieving experimental breakthrough in magical research (+2 Flow)

**Flow Expenditure:**
- Casting formulated spells (costs vary by spell power and complexity)
- Enhancing spell effects (+1 Flow for additional effect magnitude or duration)
- Stabilizing volatile magical phenomena (+2 Flow to control unstable energies)
- Adapting spell formulations for unusual conditions (+1 Flow for circumstantial modification)
- Countering opposing magical effects (+2 Flow for targeted dispelling or negation)

Wizards typically generate Flow through successful application of magical knowledge and careful observation, then spend it on spell casting or arcane manipulation. This pattern reflects their approach of thorough understanding followed by precise application.

### Signature Techniques

Wizards develop specialized techniques reflecting their systematic approach to magical manipulation:

**Pattern Recognition (Flow 1):** Apply analytical training to identify magical signatures, energy patterns, or arcane phenomena. Provides detailed information about magical effects, sources, or properties.

**Counterspell (Flow 2):** Analyze and disrupt opposing magical effects through precise interference patterns. More effective against spells with structures similar to those in wizard's own repertoire.

**Formulaic Enhancement (Flow 2):** Apply theoretical understanding to enhance a spell's effect, range, duration, or precision beyond its standard parameters.

**Arcane Analysis (Flow 1):** Examine magical item, location, or phenomenon to determine its properties, activation methods, or potential dangers through systematic assessment.

**Spell Adaptation (Flow 3):** Modify prepared spell to address circumstances different from standard casting conditions, applying theoretical understanding to practical necessity.

## Training & Mentorship

### Traditional Training Methods

Wizard training methodologies vary across traditions but typically incorporate these core elements:

**Foundational Theory:** Intensive study of basic magical principles before practical application, including energy patterns, component properties, formulaic structures, and safety protocols establishing essential knowledge foundation.

**Controlled Practice:** Supervised application beginning with minimal-risk cantrips and progressing to more complex spells, with careful assessment ensuring theoretical understanding matches practical capability.

**Research Methodology:** Training in observation protocols, documentation standards, experimental design, and analytical frameworks supporting independent investigation beyond established knowledge.

**Specialization Development:** Guided exploration of particular magical domains aligned with student aptitude and interest, including specialized techniques, distinctive theoretical frameworks, and domain-specific applications.

**Ethical Framework:** Varying emphasis on responsibility considerations regarding magical application, ranging from rigid ethical codes to utilitarian guidelines or tradition-specific prohibitions.

### Master-Student Relationships

The transmission of wizard traditions typically occurs through structured relationships between experienced practitioners and developing students:

**Academic Institutions:** Formalized educational environments with multiple instructors, standardized curriculum, and credential progression. Most common in urban centers with established magical traditions.

**Apprenticeship Bonds:** Direct one-on-one transmission where student assists master while receiving personalized instruction. Often involves substantial service component alongside educational elements.

**Research Collectives:** Collaborative environments where practitioners at various development stages work together on related projects, with mentorship occurring organically through project participation.

**Theoretical Lineages:** Knowledge transmission focused on particular magical theory or approach, creating intellectual heritage lines sometimes spanning generations of practitioners.

**Independent Development:** Uncommon path where exceptional individuals synthesize magical knowledge from written sources, observation, and personal experimentation without formal instruction.

### Training Progression

Most Wizard traditions recognize development stages marking progression from novice to master:

**Foundation Stage (Novice):** Focus on theoretical understanding, safe practice methods, and basic energy manipulation. Emphasis on control and safety before power or complexity.

**Application Stage (Intermediate):** Structured casting of established spells with growing repertoire, beginning specialized study in areas of aptitude or interest.

**Development Stage (Advanced):** Spell modification, adaptation to unusual circumstances, and supervised research projects extending established knowledge.

**Mastery Stage (Expert):** Original spell creation, independent research capability, and theoretical innovation within specialized domains.

**Authority Stage (Master):** Focus on knowledge transmission, theoretical framework development, and potential establishment of new magical approaches or traditions.

## Advancement Paths

### Theoretical Specialization

Many Wizards develop extraordinary capability within specific magical theory domains:

**Elementalism:** Specialized understanding of primal energy manifestations, including transformation mechanics, interaction principles, and application methodologies for elemental forces.

**Translocation Theory:** Focused expertise on spatial relationships, dimensional boundaries, and positional manipulation, supporting movement effects from simple teleportation to complex dimensional manipulation.

**Temporal Studies:** Rare specialization examining time-related magical phenomena, including perception manipulation, limited flow alteration, and theoretical paradox management.

**Mental Influence:** Specialized focus on consciousness interaction, including perception modification, thought pattern influence, and memory manipulation principles.

**Creation Dynamics:** Understanding of manifestation processes turning magical energy into physical substance, including permanence factors, complexity limitations, and essential property anchoring.

### Application Specialization

Some Wizards focus on particular practical applications rather than pure theory:

**Battle Magic:** Specialized combat applications emphasizing tactical advantage, threat neutralization, and protective countermeasures adapted to conflict situations.

**Enchantment Craft:** Focused expertise on embedding magical effects into physical objects, including duration maximization, trigger design, and integration optimization.

**Divinatory Practice:** Specialized information-gathering applications, including remote viewing, probability assessment, and pattern recognition methodologies.

**Restorative Magic:** Applications addressing damage or alteration reversal, including healing support, object repair, and condition neutralization.

**Environmental Manipulation:** Specialized applications affecting surroundings rather than specific targets, including area modification, condition creation, and atmosphere alteration.

### Methodological Development

Advanced Wizards often refine distinctive approaches to magical practice:

**Formula Optimization:** Development of more efficient magical patterns requiring less energy or fewer components while maintaining or enhancing effect reliability and power.

**Component Innovation:** Research into alternative magical ingredients or implementation methods reducing dependency on rare materials without compromising effect quality.

**Interdisciplinary Integration:** Incorporation of principles from non-magical knowledge domains creating innovative approaches beyond traditional arcane methodology.

**Tradition Synthesis:** Comparative analysis of different magical traditions identifying complementary elements that can be integrated into more effective hybrid approaches.

**Theoretical Modeling:** Development of improved explanatory frameworks enhancing prediction capability for complex magical interactions and previously unpredictable effects.
